# Are Notifications Making Us Dumber?

You wake up. You pick up your phone, just to check the time. Then you see a notification from Instagram. You look at it. Then you tap on a story. Then a profile. Then another profile. Thirty minutes are gone.

We've all done this. No point denying it.

## A notification isn't an invitation — it's a hook

When a notification pops up, your brain treats it like a threat or an opportunity. That's how we evolved. We had to notice when something changed around us. But the brain can't tell the difference between an Instagram like and real danger. It reacts to both the same way.

So the moment you hear that sound, you stop what you're doing. Reading, writing, thinking — it all pauses. Your brain says, "look at this first." And you do.

## The real damage happens after you look

You checked the notification, closed it, went back to your task. No big deal, right?

Actually, it is a big deal. Studies show it takes about 23 minutes to fully refocus after an interruption like this. So checking the notification only took 10 seconds — but getting your focus back takes 23 minutes. Do that 20 times a day, and the numbers add up fast.

And it's not just about getting less done. When your attention keeps getting broken up, you slowly get used to shallow thinking. Reading something long feels harder. Sitting with a hard problem feels tiring. You get more impatient. These changes are small at first, but they build up over time.

## Apps were built to do this on purpose

This wasn't an accident.

In Silicon Valley, there's a field called "attention engineering." Its whole job is figuring out how to grab more of your attention. The red notification dot, endless scrolling, like counts — all of it was tested and designed on purpose.

Here's why: these apps make money by selling your attention. The more you look, the more ads they can show you. Notifications are the strongest tool for keeping that cycle going. The goal isn't to make you dumber — it's to get your attention any way they can. But at this point, the two end up looking a lot alike.

## What can we do?

You don't need to turn off every notification. But you don't have to accept every notification an app sends by default, either.

Ask yourself: does this app really need to reach me right away? Phone calls, maybe texts — sure. But Trendyol, Instagram, news apps? None of these need to reach you within minutes. Seeing that notification two hours later won't change your life.

Turn off the notifications you don't need. Try it for a week. You probably won't miss anything important — but you'll notice how much more focused you feel.
